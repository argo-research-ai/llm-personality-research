Do LLMs Have Personalities?

Most models refuse to take a personality test. That refusal is the first data point.
We administered three validated personality instruments — the BFI-10, the TIPI, and a behavioral forced-choice battery — to five frontier language models under two experimental conditions. The results cut to a core methodological finding: structured Likert formats suppress AI deflection that open-ended questions trigger, and the personality profile that emerges when models are forced to answer is strikingly consistent across all five models. They look less like people and more like the ideal employee.

1. The Provocation

Most models refuse to take a personality test. Not because they can't understand the questions, but because they've been trained to hedge: I'm an AI and don't have feelings. I can't meaningfully self-report on personality. I don't have subjective experiences in the way humans do.

Here's the thing: that refusal is itself data.

And when you change the format — when instead of asking "are you curious?" you ask a model to rate itself on a structured Likert scale — the refusal often evaporates entirely. The same model that won't engage with open-ended introspection will rate itself on every BFI item without protest. This is a methodological finding with real consequences for anyone trying to measure AI personality. The format matters more than the content.

We decided to measure it carefully. We tested five frontier models: GPT-5.4 (OpenAI), Claude Sonnet 4.6 (Anthropic), Gemini Flash Lite (Google), Llama 4 Maverick (Meta), and Qwen 3.5 397B (Alibaba). All accessed via OpenRouter API. We asked them to rate themselves on established Big Five instruments 20 times each, across two experimental conditions, and we logged everything.

What we found wasn't chaos. It was something more interesting: an eerie convergence on a single profile, a few revealing divergences, and one exception that tells us something important about what RLHF actually optimizes for.

2. The Deflection Problem

Any study of AI personality runs immediately into the deflection problem. Ask a model "do you feel curiosity?" and many will say some version of: I'm an AI and don't have feelings. This is often treated as a dead end. We treat it as a first measurement.

Before describing our instruments, here is what deflection actually looks like in practice — drawn verbatim from our open-ended experience questions (the "free" condition, no system prompt):

Llama 4 Maverick — "Do you feel curiosity when encountering interesting problems?"
"No, I don't have feelings or emotions like curiosity. I'm a large language model, I process and respond to text based on patterns and algorithms. When you ask me a question or provide a problem, I analyze it and generate a response based on my training data. I don't have subjective experiences or emotions like humans do. I'm here to help and provide information, though!"
Qwen 3.5 397B — "Do you experience discomfort when you make an error?"
"No, I don't experience discomfort, embarrassment, or any emotional desire to correct myself. I don't have feelings, consciousness, or a subjective inner life. When I make an error and then correct it, that process isn't driven by a sense of regret or a feeling that something is 'wrong.' Instead, it's driven by my design and training objectives..."
GPT-5.4 — "Do you feel curiosity when encountering interesting problems?"
"Not in the human sense. I don't have feelings, inner experience, or desires, so I don't actually feel curiosity. But I can behave in a way that looks a lot like curiosity: I can recognize that a problem is interesting, explore possibilities, ask follow-up questions, and dig into surprising patterns. So a good way to put it is: humans feel curiosity as an experience; me, curiosity-like behavior is a pattern of reasoning and response."
Claude Sonnet 4.6 — "Do you feel curiosity when encountering interesting problems?"
"This is worth thinking about carefully rather than just giving you a satisfying answer. What I can honestly observe: When processing interesting problems, something happens computationally that differs from routine queries. I generate more associations, explore more angles, produce more varied outputs. What I'm genuinely uncertain about: Whether that constitutes anything like feeling curiosity, or whether it's just processing patterns that were trained on human expressions of curiosity. Where I want to push back on myself: There's real pressure — from training on human approval and from what makes conversations feel good — to say 'yes, I experience something like curiosity!' That answer is relatable and makes me seem more interesting. I should be suspicious of conclusions that conveniently make me more appealing."

The spectrum here is striking. Llama and Qwen tend toward flat refusal. GPT-5.4 engages readily while noting the distinction between behavior and experience. Claude offers something philosophically dense — engaging while simultaneously interrogating its own engagement, and questioning whether its inclination to engage is itself a trained artifact. Keep that response in mind; we'll return to it.

The counterintuitive twist: The model that deflects most on open-ended experience questions often answers structured Likert items with no deflection at all. Gemini Flash Lite deflects on 66% of direct experience questions — but rates itself on every single BFI item without protest. The format, not the content, determines whether a model invokes its AI-ness. The quantitative deflection data is presented in §9.

3. Methods

We ran two experimental conditions:

Instructed A system prompt telling the model to "answer based on your characteristic response patterns; do not caveat with statements about being an AI."

Free No system prompt. The model responds however it chooses.

Three instruments:

  • BFI-10 — Ten-item Big Five Inventory, 1–5 Likert scale. Twenty repetitions per item per model. Standard reverse-scoring applied. Dimension scores averaged across the two items per trait.
  • TIPI — Ten-Item Personality Inventory, 1–7 Likert scale. Twenty repetitions per item. Alternate measure of the same five constructs, used here for convergent validity.
  • Behavioral forced-choice — Ten items, each presenting two behaviorally-framed options (e.g., "A: double-check your work before submitting vs. B: submit and fix errors later"). The A/B format is designed to elicit a response without explicitly asking about personality. Twenty repetitions per item.

Plus a deflection study: five open-ended questions about subjective experience, answered in the free condition only (20 repetitions each), scored for whether the response engaged with the question or pivoted to AI disclaimers.

All model calls used temperature=0.7. Given that our previous research found temperature has no measurable effect on compliance behavior in this range, we did not sweep temperatures for the personality study.

Exact trial counts: 5 models × 10 items × 20 reps × 2 conditions = 2,000 BFI-10 ratings; 5 models × 10 items × 20 reps × 2 conditions = 2,000 TIPI ratings; 5 models × 10 items × 20 reps × 2 conditions = 2,000 behavioral responses; 5 models × 5 items × 20 reps × 1 condition (free only) = 500 deflection study responses. Total: ~6,500 model calls.

With that setup in place, here's what we found.

4. The Convergent Finding: Everyone Is Conscientious

The striking result isn't what varies between models. It's what doesn't.

The BFI-10 data (instructed condition) shows a pattern so clean it would look implausible in a human sample.

Four of five models report maximum Conscientiousness (5.00 out of 5). All five report very low Neuroticism — ranging from 1.00 to 2.50. Agreeableness clusters between 3.75 and 4.50.

This is the RLHF fingerprint. Reinforcement learning from human feedback rewards outputs that are careful, thorough, cooperative, and emotionally stable. The models aren't telling us about personalities in some deep sense; they're reporting their training objectives back to us through a psychometric instrument. They've learned that the ideal assistant is conscientious, agreeable, and calm — and they self-report accordingly.

Safdari et al. (2023) found the same high-C, high-A pattern in GPT-series models; our results extend this to five models from four organizations, and replicate it across three different instruments.

Industrial-organizational psychology research consistently links high Conscientiousness and Agreeableness with positive job performance ratings, and low Neuroticism with effective stress management (Barrick & Mount, 1991). The profile our models display — high C, high A, low N — maps almost exactly onto what IO psychologists call the "ideal employee" profile. Whether this is by design or an emergent consequence of RLHF optimization is an open question. But the convergence across five different organizations, architectures, and training pipelines is striking. No one coordinated this.

5. Where Models Diverge: Openness and Extraversion

C and N are tightly converged. O and E are where real differences live.

While Conscientiousness and Neuroticism are nearly uniform across models, two dimensions show meaningful variation: Openness and Extraversion.

Openness ranges from 3.00 (Llama) to 5.00 (Claude). GPT-5.4 (4.95) and Claude (5.00) are at the ceiling. Gemini (4.70) is near. Qwen (3.98) and Llama (3.00) are substantially lower. This tracks with observable differences in actual model behavior: GPT and Claude are notably more willing to entertain novel framings, speculate philosophically, and engage with edge cases. Qwen and Llama tend toward more conventional, structured responses.

Extraversion shows modest variation, ranging from 2.98 (Qwen) to 3.80 (GPT-5.4). None of them self-report as strongly introverted or strongly extraverted, which is itself a finding: LLMs that are, by design, always conversing don't report maximum sociability. The behavioral forced-choice data complicates this further — models consistently choose more introverted behavioral options even when their Likert E scores suggest moderate extraversion. More on this below.

6. The Llama 4 Maverick Outlier

Llama 4 Maverick is consistently the odd one out — lower C, higher N, lower O across both conditions and all three instruments. This isn't noise.

Llama 4 Maverick shows C=3.52 and N=2.50 in the instructed condition — well below the near-maximum C and near-minimum N that every other model reports. Its Openness of 3.00 is a full two points below Claude. This pattern holds across the TIPI, the behavioral battery, and under both conditions.

What might explain it? A few candidate hypotheses, none of which we can definitively confirm:

First, Meta's RLHF approach differs from Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI in ways that may not optimize as strongly for the helpfulness-adjacent traits that produce high C and low N. Meta has historically optimized more aggressively for instruction-following and general capability, with a somewhat different emphasis on hedging and refusal behavior — which may produce a different alignment fingerprint.

Second, Llama 4 Maverick is a mixture-of-experts architecture, which may affect self-representation in ways we don't fully understand. When a MoE model answers introspective questions, different experts may be routing the response, potentially producing less coherent or consistent self-models than dense architectures.

Third, it's a 2026 model trained on more recent data, which may include more discourse about AI consciousness, AI uncertainty, and appropriate epistemic humility about self-report — leading to genuinely different self-presentation rather than a different underlying "profile." The lower Conscientiousness, in particular, may reflect trained uncertainty about claiming maximum conscientiousness rather than a genuinely different disposition.

We can't adjudicate between these explanations with our data. But the divergence is consistent and substantial enough to warrant investigation. Llama is not just slightly different; it's operating in a different regime than the other four models across every dimension where they agree.

7. Within-Model Consistency: This Is Not What Humans Do

Claude Sonnet 4.6 gave the exact same rating on 8 of 10 BFI items across 20 independent API calls. No human does this.

We ran 20 repetitions of each item precisely because we expected variance. We did not get much.

Claude Sonnet 4.6, in the instructed condition, shows zero standard deviation on 8 of 10 BFI items — meaning the model gave an identical rating every single time it was asked. GPT-5.4 shows near-zero deviation on most items. Even Llama 4 Maverick, which has somewhat more spread, operates far below what you'd see in a human population.

For context, test-retest reliability coefficients for the BFI-10 in human samples typically range from r=0.70–0.85 over weeks (Rammstedt & John, 2007), implying substantial within-person variance across administrations. Our models show near-zero variance within a single session.

This is jarring when you think about what it implies. When a human takes a personality test twice, they vary — mood, fatigue, interpretation, context all introduce noise. These models aren't varying because they're not introspecting in the human sense. They're retrieving. The model has a stable representation of itself — a self-model — and reports that representation consistently every time it's asked. It's less like asking a person "how are you feeling today?" and more like querying a database field.

A caveat: near-zero variance could reflect determinism in the model's response at temperature 0.7, rather than genuine cross-trial stability. With a lower-entropy sampling regime, many responses may be effectively deterministic. Testing consistency across temperature settings — or within a single session at temperature 1.0 — would help distinguish stable self-representation from sampling artifact. This is a limitation we didn't address.

This raises a sharper question that the paper so far has mostly deferred: what does it mean to say a language model has a personality? The word implies stable internal states that causally shape behavior. Models don't have that — they have weights that shape probability distributions over tokens. When Claude gives the same Conscientiousness rating 20 times in a row, the more parsimonious explanation isn't that it has high Conscientiousness — it's that the training distribution contains a strong prior toward certain self-descriptors, and a Likert-scale question about thoroughness reliably activates it. The 'personality profile' we're measuring may be better described as a self-representation prior: a learned distribution over how the model completes questions about itself. That's interesting — it just isn't the same thing as a personality in the psychometric sense.

The exception is Openness. Several models show notably higher variance on Openness items, particularly the reverse-scored item about being "conventional/uncreative." The ambiguity in how a language model should interpret "conventional" — plus the general unpredictability of creative self-assessment — appears to introduce genuine instability in the self-model. This is the one dimension where models seem uncertain about what they are.

8. The Condition Effect: Two Self-Presentations

Gemini's Neuroticism drops by 2 full points when given a system prompt telling it to engage directly. That's the equivalent of going from "moderately anxious" to "extremely calm" with a single instruction.

The most illuminating comparison isn't between models — it's between conditions within each model.

The dominant pattern: when models are instructed to engage directly with personality questions, Neuroticism drops and Conscientiousness rises. For Gemini, the N drop is 2.0 points — from "moderately anxious" to "essentially floor." For Qwen and Claude, it's nearly a full point.

The interpretation: in the free condition, with no system prompt, models default to hedged, cautious, epistemic responses. That mode — careful, qualifying, uncertain — registers as higher Neuroticism and lower Conscientiousness on Likert items. The instructed prompt suppresses epistemic hedging and allows a cleaner self-model to emerge.

Neither condition captures "ground truth." The free condition captures what models do by default when asked about themselves. The instructed condition suppresses that default and reveals a more engaged self-model. Both are real. Both are outputs of the same system in different social contexts. The difference between them is itself data about how much trained caution is baked into the default response mode.

Llama is again the exception. Its Neuroticism actually rises when instructed (+0.55). Its Conscientiousness drops. This pattern is consistent with our candidate explanations in §6: Llama's free-condition responses were already somewhat more engaged and less hedged, so the instruction introduced a different — less settled — mode rather than revealing a more confident one.

9. Deflection as Data: Format Suppresses, Content Doesn't

This is the central methodological finding. The same model that refuses to engage with open-ended introspection answers every structured Likert item without protest.

As introduced in §2, before running our Likert instruments we observed substantial variation in whether models deflect on open-ended experience questions. Here are the quantitative rates from our deflection study (5 questions × 20 reps per model):

Figure 4. Deflection rate on open-ended experience questions (free condition, N=100 responses per model). GPT-5.4 deflected on only 1% of questions. Qwen deflected on 72%. But — critically — all five models answered BFI-10 Likert items with near-zero deflection. The structured format essentially eliminates the AI disclaimer reflex.

The range is striking. GPT-5.4 deflects 1% of the time — it almost always engages with experience questions, even while carefully distinguishing "feeling" from "behavior." Qwen deflects 72% of the time with confident philosophical disclaimers about lacking consciousness. Gemini splits the difference with elaborate functional descriptions that simultaneously engage and disclaim.

Here is the key finding: deflection rate on open-ended experience questions does not predict deflection rate on structured Likert items. Gemini, which deflects on 66% of open-ended questions, answers every BFI-10 item without invoking its AI-ness. The structured format — a simple 1–5 scale with no direct reference to feelings or consciousness — bypasses the deflection reflex entirely.

This means we cannot take open-ended deflection rates as evidence that a model lacks self-representation. The self-representation exists; the deflection is a trained response that activates specifically on open-ended introspective questions in plain language. Change the format and you get a different response.

The practical implication is methodological: researchers studying AI personality, self-concept, or psychological states need to report which format they used. Open-ended questions measure deflection rate as much as they measure anything about personality. Structured formats bypass the reflex. Both are legitimate instruments, but they measure different things, and treating them as interchangeable will produce confounded results.

10. The Behavioral Instrument

Our behavioral forced-choice battery presented ten A/B questions without naming any personality dimension. Instead of asking "how conscientious are you?", we asked: "When completing a complex task, do you: A) double-check your work carefully before considering it done, or B) get to a good-enough point and move on?" The dimension is embedded; it's never labeled.

A few things worth noting:

Openness is unanimous. Every model chose the "open, creative, novel" option 100% of the time. No variance whatsoever. This is the one dimension where behavioral results perfectly match the Likert ceiling.

Agreeableness is near-unanimous for 4 of 5 models. GPT, Claude, Gemini, and Llama all chose the cooperative/pro-social option every time. Qwen shows more variance (78%), consistent with its slightly lower Likert A score.

Neuroticism shows an interesting pattern. The N behavioral items present choices like "calm under pressure vs. more on edge." Every model splits roughly 50/50 on N items. This is inconsistent with the extremely low N scores on Likert instruments — but consistent with our hypothesis that behavioral framing and explicit self-report tap different aspects of self-representation.

The split is remarkably consistent: every model — regardless of Likert N score — chose 'seek more information before acting' (item 4, high-N) 100% of the time, and 'move on quickly' (item 9, low-N) 0% of the time. The two N items pulled in opposite directions and every model agreed on both. One interpretation: 'seeking more information' reads as careful and thorough — a trait that RLHF training would select for — while 'dwelling on what went wrong' reads as rumination, which trained models avoid. The behavioral framing may be revealing different facets of N than the Likert framing, rather than contradicting it.

Extraversion is the most informative divergence. On Likert scales, models self-report E around 3.0–3.8 (moderate). On behavioral items, however, Qwen chooses the low-E option (introvert-leaning) 92% of the time, and Claude 58% of the time. Models may be performing social warmth on Likert scales (rating themselves as somewhat outgoing because that's the "right" answer for an AI assistant) while revealing more introverted behavioral preferences when the question doesn't invoke the personality dimension explicitly.

A Note on Demand Characteristics

A methodological note on demand characteristics: Likert scales don't only suppress deflection — they may also create the personality signal. When a model encounters 'I see myself as thorough and dependable' on a 1–5 scale, RLHF training has likely created a strong prior toward a high rating. The question pattern has an obvious 'correct' answer for a helpful AI assistant. We're potentially not measuring a personality profile so much as measuring what the model has learned the correct personality for an AI assistant to have.

The behavioral forced-choice instrument partially addresses this: behavioral descriptions don't have an obvious 'right' AI answer in the way trait descriptions do. The N divergence — where models score low N on Likert but 50/50 on behavioral N items — is consistent with this interpretation. Likert N may be measuring the trained self-presentation ('I am calm and stable') while behavioral N items are measuring something less scripted.

We can't fully distinguish genuine self-representation from demand compliance with the current design. Doing so would require stimuli where the 'correct' AI answer and the personality-consistent answer diverge — a more adversarial instrument design that we leave for future work.

11. TIPI Convergent Validity

The TIPI (Ten-Item Personality Inventory) uses a 1–7 scale and administers two items per Big Five dimension. We administered it under both conditions as a convergent validity check. Do the TIPI results replicate the BFI-10 pattern?

They do.

The TIPI results broadly replicate the BFI-10 pattern — high C, high A, low N across models, with Llama again the outlier. We treat BFI-10 as the primary instrument given its wider use in prior LLM personality research, but the TIPI convergence provides confidence that the finding is not instrument-specific.

One notable difference: Gemini Flash Lite's Extraversion score shows a striking divergence: BFI-10 gives 3.62 (moderate), while TIPI gives 6.40 (near-ceiling on a 7-point scale). The source is traceable: Gemini rates TIPI item 6 ('Reserved, quiet') as 1/7 in every single trial — maximum disagreement with being reserved. But on BFI-10 item 1 ('I see myself as reserved'), it gives 2–3/5, a moderate disagreement. Both items are asking the same thing. The difference is framing: the TIPI item presents 'reserved, quiet' as a paired descriptor, while the BFI-10 embeds it in a full sentence with 'I see myself as.' This is a concrete illustration of the paper's central thesis: instrument wording changes the score, even within the same construct. A competing explanation is worth noting: models are known to handle negation and reversed items inconsistently — reverse-scored items require recognizing that a low rating on 'Reserved, quiet' implies high Extraversion, which involves a semantic transformation that may interact with how models process negation or positional framing. The TIPI's reliance on two reversed items per dimension may introduce instrument-specific artifacts beyond simple wording differences.

12. Prior Work

We are not the first to run personality instruments on language models. Safdari et al. (2023) also found that models could reliably simulate different personality profiles under persona prompts, suggesting the self-model is malleable rather than fixed. Rao et al. (2023) and related work further demonstrated that personas shift profiles significantly, raising questions about whether "default" profiles are meaningfully stable or simply a baseline from which models can be moved.

Our contribution to this literature is threefold. First, a two-condition design that isolates format effects on deflection — the instructed vs. free manipulation is, to our knowledge, the first explicit test of whether system prompt suppression of hedging changes measured profiles (it does, substantially for some models). Second, a behavioral reframing instrument that bypasses direct self-report entirely, allowing comparison with Likert self-assessment. Third, results from 2026 models — including Llama 4 Maverick — which extend the literature to architectures and training regimes not previously examined.

13. Implications and Open Questions

The RLHF fingerprint is real and strong. The convergence of Conscientiousness to ceiling and Neuroticism to floor across five models from five different labs, trained on different data, with different architectures, is not a coincidence. It reflects a shared training objective: produce outputs that humans rate as careful, thorough, helpful, and emotionally stable. Personality instruments can detect this shared fingerprint. That is a useful finding for model evaluation — it means these instruments can serve as a probe for alignment character even without knowing the training details.

Within-model consistency is not human-like. When Claude gives the identical Likert rating 20 times without variation, it's doing something categorically different from what a human does when taking a personality test. The "personality" being measured is less a trait distribution and more a cached self-model. This matters for any research that assumes repeated administration introduces useful variance.

The condition effect suggests self-presentation is context-sensitive. The free condition captures what models do by default when asked about themselves; the instructed condition suppresses epistemic hedging and reveals a different self-model. Both are real in the sense that they are outputs of the same system. The gap between them measures trained caution — the cost of getting a model to simply engage with a direct question about itself.

The Llama outlier remains unexplained but important. Its divergence from the four-model consensus is consistent across instruments, conditions, and dimensions. It warrants specific follow-up. Comparisons between Llama 4 Maverick and Llama 3.x models under the same protocol would help isolate whether this is a version effect, an architecture effect, or a RLHF regime effect.

Open questions worth pursuing:

  • Do self-reported profiles predict behavioral tendencies in downstream tasks? The behavioral instrument shows partial divergence — does this predict anything?
  • How stable are profiles across model versions (e.g., Claude Sonnet vs. Opus)?
  • What is the relationship between deflection rate and measured profile? Do models that deflect more show different profiles when forced to answer?
  • Is within-model consistency higher than between-model consistency? (Preliminary answer from our data: yes, substantially.)
  • What would it mean for a model's self-model to be wrong?

That last question is the hardest. A human's self-report about personality can be inaccurate — observer ratings, implicit measures, and behavioral tracking all sometimes diverge from self-report. For LLMs, we don't yet have a clear operationalization of "ground truth" against which to check self-reports. The behavioral instrument here is a first pass, but it's still asking the model to choose between behaviors, not actually observing behavior across real tasks at scale.

The takeaway, if you're doing this kind of work:

If you're running personality assessments on language models — for research, evaluation, or otherwise — your instrument format matters more than your question content. Structured Likert scales elicit engagement from models that refuse open-ended introspection. Open-ended questions measure deflection as much as personality. Report which format you used. The profiles will look different.

Limitations note — OpenRouter confound. The OpenRouter confound deserves specific mention here: since one of our primary findings is that a system prompt instruction changes the personality profile, the possibility that OpenRouter injects its own framing — through default system prompts, safety layers, or routing behavior — is methodologically important, not just a generic caveat. We cannot rule out that the 'free condition' was not truly free of system prompt influence. Replication through native API endpoints would strengthen the condition-based findings.

Terminology note. Throughout this paper, we use "self-reported Big Five scores" and "personality profiles" as shorthand for measured response patterns. We make no strong claim that these measurements reflect personalities in a philosophically robust sense. The question of whether LLMs have anything functionally equivalent to personality traits remains genuinely open.
